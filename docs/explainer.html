01 / THE BIG CHOICE Why we don't just use "an AI that predicts traffic"

Two ways to predict a city. We picked the one that can handle surprises.

This is the single most important idea in the whole project, so it's worth slowing down on.

The memory way

Show an AI millions of past traffic days. It learns the patterns and recalls what a typical Tuesday looks like.

  • Great at repeating days it has seen before.
  • Ask it something brand-new — “close King St for the World Cup” — and it has nothing to remember. It can only echo the past.
  • A planner's whole job is the new question. So this fails exactly when it matters.

The simulation way ← OURS

Model the actual rules — people need to get places, cars fill roads, roads have limits — and let the traffic play itself out, like a physics engine for a city.

  • Because it works from rules, it can answer questions that have never happened.
  • Change one thing and the whole city re-figures itself from scratch.
  • This is how real transportation agencies actually do it.
◆ Think of it like this

Predicting where a thrown ball lands. You could memorize ten thousand past throws — and be useless on a throw you've never seen. Or you could understand gravity, and nail any throw. flowTO chose gravity.

02 / THE ENGINE How the model figures out traffic

It answers four simple questions, in order.

Planners call this the “four-step model.” It's the same recipe agencies have used for decades — we just run it fast, on the whole city at once.

1

“How many people are heading out right now?”

Depends on the time and where homes, jobs, and big draws sit. At 5 p.m., offices empty out. On match day, 45,000 people pour out of BMO Field at once.

2

“Where is each of them going?”

People tend to head to places that are big and close. A huge mall pulls more trips than a corner store; somewhere far pulls fewer than somewhere near. (Planners literally call this a “gravity” model — bigger places have more pull.)

3

“Car, transit, bike, or walk?”

Each person picks whatever's fastest and easiest for them. Make driving miserable and some switch to the streetcar. Make transit faster and more pile on. This is why shutting a subway line floods the buses nearby.

4

“Which exact roads and lines do they fill?”

Everyone takes the route they think is fastest. Pile enough cars onto a road and it clogs — so the next drivers spill onto side streets. The model tracks how full every single road gets. That fullness is the heatmap.

…and here's the twist that makes it real: the clog from question 4 changes the answers to 2 and 3. So the model loops back and asks again.

03 / THE KEY IDEA The part most people miss

Traffic settles into a balance — and so does the model.

Real drivers avoid jams. If everyone took the “best” road, it would instantly become the worst road — so people reshuffle until no single driver could get there faster by switching. That settled state is called balance (planners say “equilibrium”).

flowTO does the same dance: it runs the four questions, sees where it clogged, lets people re-route around the clog, and runs them again — over and over — until the picture stops moving. That stable picture is the prediction.

◆ Think of it like this

Checkout lines at a grocery store. People drift to the shortest line until every line is about equally slow. Nobody planned it — it just balances out. A whole city of drivers does exactly that, and the model finds where it lands.

05 / WORKED EXAMPLE Why one small change matters everywhere

Close King Street at 5 p.m. — follow the dominoes.

DOMINO 01

The cars don't vanish.

King is closed, but those drivers still need to get home. They spill onto Queen and Front.

DOMINO 02

Those streets go red.

Queen and Front were already busy. The extra cars tip them into gridlock — the heatmap lights up.

DOMINO 03

Some drivers give up.

Seeing the mess, a chunk of them abandon the car and hop the 504 streetcar instead.

DOMINO 04

A new balance — with a number.

The streetcar gets crowded, the roads settle, and flowTO reports it: “average trip +6 min.”

No human placed any of that. You changed one thing — King is closed — and the model worked out all four dominoes by itself. Do this for a whole World Cup, and a planner can see trouble coming weeks before it arrives.

06 / WHY YOU CAN TRUST IT Boring on purpose

Same question in, same answer out. Every time.

Run the exact same scenario twice and you get the exact same result — down to the last street. flowTO is a calculator, not a slot machine.

That matters for two reasons. A planner can stand in front of city council and defend a number that won't change on a re-run. And on a demo stage, nothing surprises us — what we rehearsed is what happens.

◆ Think of it like this

Press 7 × 8 on a calculator and you always get 56. You'd never trust a calculator that sometimes said 55. Planning decisions deserve the same.

09 / THE TWO AI HELPERS On top of the simulation, not instead of it

One to talk to. One to do the searching for you.

The copilot — talk to the city

Type a plain-English wish. It sets up the scenario, runs the model, and explains the result back to you — no menus, no jargon.

Ease the post-match gridlock around BMO Field without breaking any bylaws.
Tried 3 fixes. Best: contraflow on Lakeshore + 50% more 509 streetcars. Egress time −32%, $0 cost, no bylaws broken.

The optimizer — find the best fix

Instead of you guessing, it quietly tries thousands of combinations — reroutes, lane changes, transit boosts — and hands you the ones that work best, within the rules and the budget.

It can only do this because the simulation gives it a trustworthy score for every idea it tries. The model is the judge; the optimizer is the contestant.

Plain-words dictionary

The jargon, decoded.

Digital twin
A living virtual copy of the real city you can experiment on safely.
Simulation
Letting the rules play out to see what happens — vs. recalling what happened before.
Four-step model
The four questions: how many trips, going where, by what mode, on which roads.
Gravity model
The idea that bigger, closer places attract more trips — like gravitational pull.
Mode choice
Whether each person drives, takes transit, bikes, or walks.
Equilibrium / balance
The settled state where no driver could get there faster by switching routes.
Capacity
How many cars a road can carry before it clogs.
Deterministic
Same inputs always give the same answer — like a calculator.
Heatmap
The colour overlay: green = flowing, red = jammed.
Optimizer
The helper that auto-searches for the best fix under your rules and budget.
