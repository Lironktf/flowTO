00 / THE GIST The whole thing in one breath

It's SimCity — but it's the real Toronto, and it's a serious tool.

A city planner's job is full of expensive, irreversible decisions: close this street, reroute that bus, add a bike lane. flowTO lets them test those decisions on a virtual city first and see the knock-on effects across every road and transit line.

A copy of the city

Every street, every TTC line, GO, and UP Express — laid out in 3-D with building outlines and a congestion heatmap on top.

Set the clock

Pick a time of day and date. A weekday 5 p.m. looks nothing like a Sunday morning — the model knows the difference.

Pull the levers

Close a road, drop a lane, add a crowd, boost a streetcar. Each change ripples through the whole city, and you watch it happen.

01 / THE BIG CHOICE Why we don't just use "an AI that predicts traffic"

Two ways to predict a city. We picked the one that can handle surprises.

This is the single most important idea in the whole project, so it's worth slowing down on.

The memory way

Show an AI millions of past traffic days. It learns the patterns and recalls what a typical Tuesday looks like.

  • Great at repeating days it has seen before.
  • Ask it something brand-new — “close King St for the World Cup” — and it has nothing to remember. It can only echo the past.
  • A planner's whole job is the new question. So this fails exactly when it matters.

The simulation way ← OURS

Model the actual rules — people need to get places, cars fill roads, roads have limits — and let the traffic play itself out, like a physics engine for a city.

  • Because it works from rules, it can answer questions that have never happened.
  • Change one thing and the whole city re-figures itself from scratch.
  • This is how real transportation agencies actually do it.
◆ Think of it like this

Predicting where a thrown ball lands. You could memorize ten thousand past throws — and be useless on a throw you've never seen. Or you could understand gravity, and nail any throw. flowTO chose gravity.

02 / THE ENGINE How the model figures out traffic

It answers four simple questions, in order.

Planners call this the “four-step model.” It's the same recipe agencies have used for decades — we just run it fast, on the whole city at once.

1

“How many people are heading out right now?”

Depends on the time and where homes, jobs, and big draws sit. At 5 p.m., offices empty out. On match day, 45,000 people pour out of BMO Field at once.

2

“Where is each of them going?”

People tend to head to places that are big and close. A huge mall pulls more trips than a corner store; somewhere far pulls fewer than somewhere near. (Planners literally call this a “gravity” model — bigger places have more pull.)

3

“Car, transit, bike, or walk?”

Each person picks whatever's fastest and easiest for them. Make driving miserable and some switch to the streetcar. Make transit faster and more pile on. This is why shutting a subway line floods the buses nearby.

4

“Which exact roads and lines do they fill?”

Everyone takes the route they think is fastest. Pile enough cars onto a road and it clogs — so the next drivers spill onto side streets. The model tracks how full every single road gets. That fullness is the heatmap.

…and here's the twist that makes it real: the clog from question 4 changes the answers to 2 and 3. So the model loops back and asks again.

03 / THE KEY IDEA The part most people miss

Traffic settles into a balance — and so does the model.

Real drivers avoid jams. If everyone took the “best” road, it would instantly become the worst road — so people reshuffle until no single driver could get there faster by switching. That settled state is called balance (planners say “equilibrium”).

flowTO does the same dance: it runs the four questions, sees where it clogged, lets people re-route around the clog, and runs them again — over and over — until the picture stops moving. That stable picture is the prediction.

Under the hood this is a textbook method: a BPR road-speed curve (a road slows as it fills) driving a Frank-Wolfe user-equilibrium solver that stops when the “relative gap” closes. Same math the big transport agencies use.

◆ Think of it like this

Checkout lines at a grocery store. People drift to the shortest line until every line is about equally slow. Nobody planned it — it just balances out. A whole city of drivers does exactly that, and the model finds where it lands.

06 / WHY YOU CAN TRUST IT Boring on purpose

Same question in, same answer out. Every time.

Run the exact same scenario twice and you get the exact same result — down to the last street. flowTO is a calculator, not a slot machine.

That matters for two reasons. A planner can stand in front of city council and defend a number that won't change on a re-run. And on a demo stage, nothing surprises us — what we rehearsed is what happens.

◆ Think of it like this

Press 7 × 8 on a calculator and you always get 56. You'd never trust a calculator that sometimes said 55. Planning decisions deserve the same.

08 / CLOSING THE LOOP From the whiteboard to a model that learns

Predict a lane closure — then learn from what really happened.

Section 07 proved the engine matches a textbook answer. This is the next move: point it at real Toronto closures, compare the prediction to what actually happened, and feed the gap back in until it's accurate. That loop is the whiteboard.

The whiteboard, decoded

A model learns how traffic flows on open roads, predicts what happens when a lane closes, compares that to reality, and uses the delta to correct itself. Repeat → it gets accurate.

◆ Think of it like this

It's how weather models earn trust: forecast tomorrow, then check the forecast against what the sky actually did, and let every miss sharpen the next one. flowTO does that — but for closing a lane on King Street.

Which model — two paths

Path A · physics + calibration ← RECOMMENDED SPINE

The predictor stays your BPR + Frank-Wolfe equilibrium. The "training arc" tunes its physical parameters — per-class α/β, edge capacities, OD demand — so predicted closure flows match observed TMC deltas. Mechanically this is the same IPF/ODME machinery you already wrote, extended from open-road counts to closures.

  • Interpretable, deterministic — great for demos and city council.
  • Already ~80% built; low risk in a hackathon window.

Path B · learned surrogate

A graph neural net (PyTorch Geometric / STGCN-style) maps [graph state + closure mask] → link-flow field, learning the residual the physics misses. Train on sim-generated closures at scale (cheap on GPU) plus real observed deltas. This is the literal "train the model" box.

  • The flashy "AI + feedback loop" story; justifies the GPU stack.
  • More risk to land in time.
→ Recommendation

Lead with Path A as the reliable spine, then bolt on a thin Path B layer — a small model that predicts the correction delta, trained against observed closures. You get the trustworthy instrument and the "we train a model in a feedback loop" narrative. In A the loop tunes parameters; in B it's the loss signal.

How the loop actually runs

STEP 01

Calibrate the baseline.

Fit OD to open-road TMC — exactly what the existing ODME pass does today.

STEP 02

Apply the real closure & predict.

Mutate the segment that actually closed, then re-solve to equilibrium (blast-radius for speed).

STEP 03

Measure the delta.

Δ = predicted_flow − observed_TMC on the segments near the closure.

STEP 04

Shrink it, repeat → trained.

Nudge capacity / α,β / OD until Δ stops improving. Held-out closures are the test set.

◆ Why it feels instant — the “blast radius”

Close one street and flowTO doesn't re-solve all of Toronto. It recomputes only the affected neighbourhood — about 1,300 roads around the change — and widens that zone automatically if the jam spreads. That's the ~84 ms; a full city re-solve still finishes in a few seconds.

The copilot — talk to the city

A local Nemotron model (running on-device through Ollama) turns a plain-English wish into a concrete plan, runs the model, and explains the result — citing the exact bylaws it stayed inside. It previews every change first; nothing is applied until you confirm.

Ask for something illegal — “just close Lake Shore both ways” — and it refuses, naming the fire-route and transit-replacement rules it would break, then offers a legal alternative.

The optimizer — find the best fix

Instead of you guessing, it searches the combinations — reroutes, lane changes, signal retimes, transit holds — and hands you the ones that work best, within the rules and the budget. A fast heuristic always returns an improving plan; NVIDIA cuOpt sharpens the constrained sub-problems on the GPU.

It can only do this because every candidate is scored by actually running the simulation — no idea is trusted until the model has run it. The simulation is the judge; the optimizer is the contestant.

How it's actually built, end to end.

Everything above is the plain-language story. Below is the engineering: the data model, the simulation math, the API contracts, and how each piece hands off to the next. Open a panel to drop down into the details.

◆ First, the data model — get this right

A road network is a directed graph. The nodes (vertices) are intersections; the edges are the street segments between them — one edge per direction. This is the opposite of a common first guess, and it matters: a street segment is the thing that fills up, so capacity, lane count, length, speed limit and the congestion math all live on edges. Nodes are just the junctions where edges meet (and where turn restrictions and signals apply). Everything downstream — routing, the BPR curve, the heatmap colour — is keyed by edge_id.

A The graph — what we expose

Built with OSMnx from OpenStreetMap (the demo-safe baseline) or from the official Toronto Centreline behind a graph_source flag — both emit the same canonical edge schema, so routing, mutations and the simulator are source-agnostic. A bidirectional street becomes two directed edges; one-way is read from OSM tags or Centreline's ONEWAY_DIR_CODE.

Canonical edge record

# every edge carries the same fields, whatever the source
edge_id, from_node, to_node,        # topology (from_node/to_node are intersections)
road_name, road_class,              # "Lake Shore Blvd W", arterial|expressway|collector|local|transit
length_m, one_way, speed_kmh, lanes,
capacity,                           # veh/hr = lanes × saturation-flow (HCM-ish defaults, TMC-calibrated)
base_time_min, current_time_min,    # free-flow vs congested travel time
status,                             # open | closed | restricted
load, pressure,                     # live volume and v/c (0=free … 1+=gridlock) — this drives the heatmap
geometry,                           # LineString [lng,lat] for the map
confidence                          # observed | inferred | default | manual, per inferred field

Nodes hold node_id, position, and act as the junctions for turn restrictions/signals. Per-edge confidence labels tell a planner which numbers came from real data vs a class default — a credibility feature, surfaced in the UI.

Mutations (how a scenario edits the network)

Scenarios never edit the baseline in place — they copy-on-mutate. The mutation primitives mirror the intervention tools:

B Demand & OD — who travels where

The simulator needs an origin–destination (OD) matrix: how many trips flow from each zone to each zone in this time slice. We build it in stages and keep the output interface dead simple.

Pipeline

  • Node demand — a weather-aware ML model (gradient-boosted, 11 features incl. time, weather, downtown distance, road class) predicts how many trips each node generates/attracts; a heuristic fallback covers the model-less path.
  • Gravity — distribute those trips: bigger, closer destinations pull more (the “gravity model”).
  • IPF / Furness — balance the matrix to production/attraction marginals so totals are right.
  • ODME — nudge it until the assigned link volumes match observed turning-movement counts (TMC), so the OD reflects reality, not just a formula.
  • Seed — the Transportation Tomorrow Survey (TTS2016R) provides the prior; Census × employment gravity is the fallback.

Zones are the 158 City neighbourhoods (or 25 wards) as TAZ proxies; graph nodes map to zones by spatial join. An event like the World Cup is just an extra production spike injected at the stadium zone.

Output interface (stable contract)

generate_od_matrix(time="Fri 17:00", calibration="ipf_counts")
  → [ {origin: node_id, destination: node_id, trips: float}, … ]
C The simulation engine — BPR + Frank-Wolfe

Given the graph and the OD matrix, the engine finds the user equilibrium: the flow on every edge where no driver could reach their destination faster by switching routes. Two ingredients.

1 · BPR volume-delay (per edge)

A road slows smoothly as it fills. t0 is free-flow time, v is volume, c is capacity:

t = t0 · (1 + α · (v/c)^β)        α = 0.15 ,  β = 4   # BPR, per road class
                                  c = 0  →  t = ∞      # a closed edge

2 · Frank-Wolfe assignment (whole network)

x ← 0 ;  t ← t0
repeat:
  for each origin zone:                # the hot, parallel loop
     SSSP tree under current t          # shortest paths (Dijkstra / cuGraph)
     load full OD onto them → aux flow y   # all-or-nothing
  λ ← line-search on the Beckmann objective    # λ in [0,1], deterministic
  x ← x + λ·(y − x) ;  t ← bpr(x)
  rgap ← (Σ t·x − Σ t·y) / (Σ t·x)
until  rgap ≤ 1e-4  or  iter ≥ 50      # bi-conjugate FW: ~3–5× fewer iters

Backends & flags

The shortest-path inner loop runs through one interface, sssp_tree(graph, origin, weight), with two backends: cpu (NetworkX) and gpu (NVIDIA cuGraph, Spark-only, smoke-gated → auto-falls back to CPU). Behaviour is selected by flags, baseline always available:

flagsafe baselinespec upgrade
enginekpathequilibrium (FW/BFW)
congestion_modellegacy lookupbpr
backendcpu (NetworkX)gpu (cuGraph)
calibrationnoneipf / ipf_counts
recomputefullblast (blast-radius)

Determinism & correctness

Fixed iteration caps + rgap target (never stop on wall-clock), float64, ordered reductions, and an edge_id tie-break epsilon kill shortest-path-tie nondeterminism — same input → identical output. Correctness is anchored by an AequilibraE oracle: on TNTP benchmark networks (SiouxFalls, Anaheim) our link flows match the reference solver within relative L∞ ≤ 1%.

D Blast-radius recompute — why edits feel instant

Re-solving all of Toronto for every tweak would be too slow to drag. Instead, when an edge changes, we recompute only the affected subgraph and widen it automatically if the jam spreads past a threshold.

Algorithm sketch

  • Find OD paths touching the changed edge set (an {edge_id → [path_id]} reverse index makes this O(1)).
  • Grow upstream + downstream cones (bounded Dijkstra/BFS), weighted by road class, current pressure, and betweenness.
  • Add near-cost parallel alternatives; always include the expressway core; freeze boundary in/out-flows from the baseline.
  • Re-run equilibrium on just that subgraph; if boundary pressure shifts > threshold, widen and repeat.

Defaults

parametervalue
start radius8 min free-flow / 2 km
max radius20 min / 6 km
widen threshold8% boundary pressure change
target latency< 100 ms (≈ 84 ms, ~1,300 edges)

Speed mode (cached alternatives) drives the live drag; accuracy mode (full affected-OD recompute) runs on release and for the final before/after compare. Same change + baseline → identical subgraph + result.

Optimizer (heuristic + cuOpt, the “Verifiers” story)

Searches the action space (reroutes, lane allocations, signal offsets, transit-frequency bumps, contraflow) under bylaw + budget constraints. I Closure-calibration loop — learning from real closures TO BUILD

The feedback loop from §08, in code terms. It generalizes the existing open-road ODME (§B) to closure scenarios: predict the flow under a closure that really happened, diff it against observed TMC, and correct the sim's parameters until the gap closes. The converged parameters are the trained model.

What to extend

  • ODME pass — generalize the count-matching step from open-road counts to closure-scenario deltas.
  • Mutations (§A) — already done: close() / set_capacity() apply the closure.
  • Blast-radius (§D) — the fast re-solve for the predict step.
  • Validation — MAE / RMSE per iteration on a held-out set of closures.
  • Data pipeline (§G) — add a closure ground-truth normalizer (TMC × known closure windows).

Data joins

  • Join key: CENTRELINE_ID ↔ TMC location ↔ Road Restrictions segment.
  • Observed delta = TMC(during closure) − TMC(typical same weekday / hour).
  • If logging forward: snapshot the live road-restrictions feed on a cron to build a closure history.

Loop shape

# calibrate sim params against one observed closure
od   = odme(seed_od, open_tmc, path_of)              # 1. baseline (existing)
g    = set_capacity(graph, closed_edge, 0.0)         # 2. apply the real closure
for it in range(MAX_ITERS):
    flow  = simulate(g, od, recompute="blast")        # 3. predict
    delta = flow.near(closed_edge) - observed_tmc       # 4. Δ
    od, g = correct(od, g, delta)                       # 5. shrink Δ (geometric-mean ratio)
    if rmse(delta) < EPS: break                     # 6. converged → trained

Plain-words dictionary

The jargon, decoded.

Digital twin
A living virtual copy of the real city you can experiment on safely.
Simulation
Letting the rules play out to see what happens — vs. recalling what happened before.
Four-step model
The four questions: how many trips, going where, by what mode, on which roads.
Gravity model
The idea that bigger, closer places attract more trips — like gravitational pull.
Mode choice
Whether each person drives, takes transit, bikes, or walks.
Equilibrium / balance
The settled state where no driver could get there faster by switching routes. flowTO reaches it with a Frank-Wolfe solver.
BPR curve
The road-speed rule (Bureau of Public Roads): a road slows down smoothly as it fills past its capacity.
Capacity
How many cars a road can carry before it clogs.
Blast radius
The neighbourhood of roads flowTO recomputes after a change — so it reacts in milliseconds, not minutes.
Deterministic
Same inputs always give the same answer — like a calculator.
AequilibraE
The trusted open-source traffic solver flowTO checks itself against on benchmark networks.
Heatmap
The colour overlay: green = flowing, red = jammed.
Nemotron
NVIDIA's language model — here it runs locally as the plain-English copilot.
cuOpt
NVIDIA's GPU optimizer; sharpens the constrained “find the best plan” search.
GTFS
The standard transit-schedule format — feeds TTC, GO, and UP Express into the model.
Optimizer
The helper that auto-searches for the best fix under your rules and budget.
ODME
Origin–Destination Matrix Estimation — nudging the trip table until the simulated road counts match the real observed counts (TMC).
TMC
Turning-Movement Counts — Toronto's intersection traffic counts in 15-minute bins; flowTO's "ground truth" for how many vehicles really moved.
Delta (Δ) / feedback loop
The gap between what the model predicted and what actually happened; feeding it back to correct the model is how it learns.
Verifiers
Prime Intellect's framework for scoring agents against a checkable reward — here, "did the prediction match reality within ε?"
